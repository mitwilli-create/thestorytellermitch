I'm Mitchell Williams, and content-ops is a production system I built to turn one idea into the right post for every place it lands. Cross-posting the same block of text everywhere is why so much content reads as noise, so this system treats each surface, Substack, LinkedIn, X, Hacker News, Reddit, Discord, GitHub, TikTok, YouTube, as its own medium with its own mechanics. It scouts live angles into a ledger, drafts a master version, then adapts that master for four distinct audiences. Every draft runs through one deterministic voice and format gate, so what counts as on-voice has one answer, not several. And nothing publishes on its own. The system drafts, stages, and proposes, then stops. I press send, every time. It's the same loop I ran for years in newsrooms and in Google's internal comms, automated wherever the work was mechanical instead of editorial.

What it is

Cross-posting one block of text everywhere is why so much content reads as noise. content-ops treats each surface as its own medium with its own mechanics, and treats voice as something to enforce, not hope for. It is a pipeline for one operator to run the work of a small content team, with the editorial judgment kept where it belongs.

Cross-posting the same text
One block of copy pasted into every feed. It fits none of them, ignores what each audience is there for, and reads as broadcast, so it gets scrolled past.
content-ops
A master idea adapted to each surface's mechanics and each audience's reason for being there, every draft checked against a deterministic voice and format gate, and nothing shipped until a person approves it.
What it does

It starts from an angle, not a blank page. A story-scout agent refreshes a ledger with live angles worth writing. A story becomes its own working directory: a master draft, one adaptation per platform, and a running notes file, so the whole life of a piece stays in one place, not scattered across tabs.

Each surface is its own medium. A platform-adapter reshapes the master to the mechanics of the target: what a Hacker News title has to do that a LinkedIn hook does not, how X threading differs from a Substack lede, what a technical subreddit will and will not tolerate. It writes for four distinct audiences, from AI developers to people newly getting their hands on these tools, because the same idea needs a different door for each.

The playbooks are dated, and go stale on purpose. The knowledge base holds four audience profiles and nine platform playbooks, and every mechanics claim carries a date. A 30-day staleness gate means the timing and adaptation steps refuse to run against a playbook that has aged out, so the system never quietly optimizes for how a platform worked last quarter. Platform mechanics move; the tool is built to admit that.

One gate, and one hard stop. Every draft passes through voice-gates.mjs, a single deterministic voice and format check that both the review step and the evaluation harness consume, so "does this sound right" has one answer, not several. And the publishing gate is absolute: the agent drafts, stages, and proposes, and then it stops. A person presses send, every time.

One story becomes one working directory: master.md, a place for each platform adaptation, and a notes file. The canonical version lives in one place, so every downstream cut traces back to it.

A platform-adapter reshapes the master to each surface's mechanics for four audiences. A title, a hook, a thread structure, and a length that fit the place it will actually be read, not one block of copy forced into every feed.

Check the timing against dated playbooks
Platform mechanics are stored with dates and a 30-day staleness gate. The timing and adaptation steps refuse to run against a playbook that has aged out, so the tool never optimizes for a version of a platform that no longer exists.
05
Gate for voice and format
Every draft passes through voice-gates.mjs, one deterministic check consumed by both the review step and the evaluation harness. The definition of on-voice and on-format is a single source, not a matter of opinion per post.
06
Human presses send
The publishing gate is absolute. The agent drafts, stages, and proposes, then stops. Nothing reaches an audience without a person approving it. The machine removes the mechanical load and leaves the editorial call where it belongs.
One story, the real run

This is an actual run from the repo, July 2026: Mike Krieger's session at the AI Engineer World's Fair, from a verbatim transcript I captured in the room to a published essay. Every artifact below exists in the drafts folder with its gate scores; nothing here is a mock-up.

Source to master. The transcript went in; the master draft came out arguing a specific editorial angle: everyone clipped the "Claude is now writing Claude" stat, and the stat wasn't the story. Master headline: "Everyone Clipped the Wrong Line From Mike Krieger."

Master to surfaces. The LinkedIn adaptation went through two drafts and a voicing pass; the long-form version became the Substack essay, retitled at the edit desk to "What Do AI Employees Know About Using LLMs That I Don't?" with the dek "Their edge isn't IQ. It's a workflow you can steal." Same reporting, two different doors, because the surfaces reward different entries.

LinkedIn, first draft
"Everyone who watched Mike Krieger at the AI Engineer World's Fair walked out quoting the same stat: Claude writes most of Anthropic's code now, north of 80 percent. Great line. It wasn't the story."
LinkedIn, voiced final
"I spent last week at the AI Engineer World's Fair, and the sharpest thing I saw wasn't a product launch. It was Mike Krieger, who built Instagram, describing how he actually works at Anthropic now."

The gate, with numbers. The essay's final voice pass scored 17 sections at an average fidelity of 0.698 (range 0.580 to 0.797), retention 1.185 against a 0.7 floor, zero em dashes, zero banned words: guard PASS. Before that pass, the pre-publish review returned partial with five named gaps, each logged, each resolved or explicitly waived with a reason.

The human edit, logged. The final edit log is part of the artifact: reworked the opening two paragraphs to name what actually flipped in Krieger's work; cut an infographic the piece didn't earn; re-bulleted a run-on list; softened two lines the review flagged as over-asserted; removed one unsourced claim while keeping the sourced one beside it. Published at 2,599 words. The essay is live; judge the taste directly.

One honesty note: this story ran to the surfaces its angle earned, the essay and LinkedIn. The playbooks cover more surfaces, and every adaptation passes the same gate; the run above is what shipping one story actually looks like, edit log and all.

Why it matters

Producing good content across many surfaces is a real operational job: sourcing, drafting, shaping each version to its medium, protecting a consistent voice, and knowing what actually earns attention on each platform this month. content-ops is that job, built as a system a single operator can run, the mechanical parts handed to the machine, the editorial parts kept human. It is the same loop I ran in newsrooms and internal communications, now with tools I built to move faster without losing the taste. And the engine points inward as easily as out: swap the platform playbooks for all-hands scripts, Slack digests, intranet features, or a podcast readout, and it is an internal-content desk.

Source the angle. Draft the master. Shape it for the room it lands in. Gate the voice. Then stop, and let a person send it. The machine carries the load; the judgment stays mine.
