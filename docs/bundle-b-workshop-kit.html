One Script, Every Market · The 30-Minute Workshop Kit

Built as part of the One Script, Seven Markets spec bundle for the ElevenLabs Forward Deployed Creative role. Cost figures are measured actuals from the bundle's logged receipts (2026-07-13) or published list rates as cited inline. MERIDIEM is a fictional canned Spanish white wine created for the demo. No company named in this document is claimed as an ElevenLabs client.

A session an account executive runs solo for a brand or agency marketing team. No engineer required. Everything the facilitator needs is in this file plus the demo assets in this bundle.

Session goal: the attendees leave having localized one line of THEIR copy into one of their markets, in a consistent brand voice, and having heard the music bed shift with it. One felt moment beats forty slides.

Room setup (before anyone arrives)

  • Laptop with ElevenLabs open, logged into a workspace where the Multi-market ad variant template is shared, credits available.
  • reel-one-script-seven-markets.mp4 downloaded locally (never demo over conference wifi streaming).
  • One attendee-facing page printed or shared: their agenda is sections 1 to 5 below without the facilitator notes.
  • Read the bundle's brand/meridiem-fact-sheet.md so obvious product questions (what grape, is it sweet, why a can) get instant answers.
  • Ask the champion beforehand for: one 2 to 4 sentence piece of the brand's real copy, their brand voice if they have one cloned (otherwise use a licensed library voice, and say so out loud), and which market they care about most.

Agenda

0:00 to 0:03 · The reel, cold

Play the reel. No preamble beyond one sentence: "One rooftop in Cordoba, one match night, one voice; watch what changes and what does not."

Facilitator talk track: "What you just saw: the film never changed and the voice never changed. The language changed, the music changed, and the crowd's shirts changed with the market, red in Spain, orange in the Netherlands, white in England. Nobody re-shot anything and nobody re-booked talent. The master cost a few dollars of generation; every market after it cost a small fraction of that, and the receipts in this kit price each one to the cent. I will happily send them."

0:03 to 0:08 · One localization decision, up close

Show the ES and DE scripts side by side (localized-scripts folder). Walk ONE line: "con la chispa justa" became "mit genau der richtigen Perlage."

Talk track: "Chispa is Spanish wine talk. A literal German translation would say Funke and sound ridiculous. Perlage is what a German wine copywriter actually writes. This is the core idea of the workshop: the tool does the production, your people still make the creative calls. We call it transcreation, and the template treats your localized copy as an input, not something a machine guesses at. The same discipline applies to picture: the rooftop crowd wears plain kit in each market's colors, no crests, no badges, because national colors are culture and federation crests are somebody's trademark."

0:08 to 0:22 · Hands on: their copy, their market

The exercise, at the front screen, driven by an attendee (not the facilitator) with the facilitator navigating verbally:

  1. Open the Multi-market ad variant template and duplicate it into a working flow.
  2. Paste their real copy into the master Text node. Run the TTS node. Play it. (First reaction moment: their words in a production voice.)
  3. Ask the room to transcreate one line into their chosen market's language. Let them argue about idiom for two minutes; that argument IS the lesson. Paste the result into the market Text node.
  4. Run the market TTS node. Play EN and the market version back to back. Same voice, two languages.
  5. Change the market Music node prompt to match their brand's register (ask the room: "what does your brand sound like in Madrid at 8pm?"). Run it. Play the two beds back to back.
  6. Hover the Run controls and show the credit estimates before running anything. Say the numbers out loud. Cost transparency is a feature of the demo, not a footnote.

Facilitator recovery paths:

  • If TTS output sounds flat: shorten the line, add punctuation for breath, rerun. Narrate the fix; iteration speed is part of the pitch.
  • If credits or access fail: fall back to the pre-produced MERIDIEM assets and narrate what each click would have done. Never end the segment without playing SOMETHING they chose.
  • If the room goes quiet at step 3: offer two candidate transcreations yourself and make them pick. Disagreement follows immediately.

0:22 to 0:27 · Objection FAQ, preempted

Facilitator raises the three big ones proactively (answers below), then opens the floor.

0:27 to 0:30 · The ask

Talk track: "Two things before we break. One: send me the campaign that is currently slowest to localize; we will run this exact exercise on it and send you the output with a receipt. Two: who on your team should own this template? I will share it to your workspace today." Book the follow-up before people stand up.

Objection FAQ (facilitator answers)

"Will the voice stay consistent with our brand across languages?"
Yes, and that is the point of using one cloned or licensed voice as the anchor: the same voice model speaks every market. Two honest caveats. Accent character can vary by language and should be reviewed by a native speaker on the team before anything ships. And voice consistency is only as good as the source: a professionally cloned brand voice from clean recordings behaves far better than a quick clone from a phone memo.

"Is this translation? Our markets hate translated ads."
No. The template deliberately does NOT auto-translate. Localized copy is written by people (your team, your agency, or a copywriter using an LLM as a drafting tool) and pasted in as a creative input. What the workflow automates is everything AFTER the creative decision: the voice session, the music adaptation, the assembly. The demo's own scripts document every adaptation decision line by line, and that document is part of the handoff.

"What are we allowed to do with the output? Whose voice is it, whose music is it?"
Four layers, keep them separate. Category rules first: if the product is alcohol, market advertising law leads everything (France's Loi Evin bans lifestyle framing outright; the demo set carries a product-only French cut for exactly this reason), and counsel signs off per market before anything airs. Then the usual three. Platform license: commercial usage rights come with paid ElevenLabs tiers, per the public pricing page; confirm the current terms for your tier when you sign. Voice rights: if the voice is cloned from a person, you need that person's consent and a talent agreement covering markets and media, exactly like a human VO buyout, and your legal team should treat it as one. Music: generated beds come from the platform under the same commercial terms; if you layer in licensed catalog music instead, your existing licensing applies. For any broadcast buy, run the specific plan and territory past counsel; do not accept a verbal answer in a workshop, including this one.

"Our compliance team will never approve AI ads." (bonus, it comes up)
The workflow feeds your existing approval chain, it does not bypass it. Every asset is reviewable before it ships, and several EU markets are moving toward AI-content disclosure norms, so build the disclosure line into the end card template once and stop thinking about it per campaign.

Facilitator checklist (print this half page)

  • Reel plays locally with sound, tested in the actual room.
  • Template shared to a workspace the demo login can reach; credits confirmed.
  • Champion's real copy received in advance; a fallback line chosen from their public site in case the copy does not arrive.
  • Brand voice or licensed voice selected; if it is a library voice, say so when it first plays.
  • localized-scripts folder open in a tab for the side-by-side moment.
  • Follow-up slot proposed before the room empties.
