How I'd run Cursor's enterprise workshop program

Written for Cursor · July 2026 · Public sources only · ~7 min read

Cursor became the fastest-scaling B2B software company on record with almost no marketing at all: $100 million to $2 billion in annualized revenue in about thirteen months. Engineers found it, trusted it, and brought it to work.

What follows is a map of the openings I see in the workshop program this role owns, and what I would do with each one in the first weeks on the job. Everything here comes from the public record. I have no inside knowledge of Cursor, and I haven't invented any.

01 · Opportunity

Bottom-up got Cursor into the building. Top-down signs the contract.

Cursor's own enterprise page counts 64 percent of the Fortune 500 and 50,000+ enterprises building with it. That happened from the bottom up: one engineer adopting, then a team, then a floor.

The enterprise contract is a different transaction. The person who standardizes an org on Cursor was usually never in the room where the tool won. They need rollout evidence, admin and security answers, and a productivity story they can defend to a CFO, and no amount of developer enthusiasm substitutes for it.

Workshops close that gap because they earn trust the same way the product spread: peers demonstrating real work, live. Cursor already runs public practitioner workshops; the opening this role owns is pointing that motion at the enterprise, deliberately. The posting for this role says it plainly: follow-up has to turn engagement into pipeline.

What I'd do

  • Build the topic engine from the field. A standing intake from Sales, Customer Success, and Product for what enterprise buyers, admins, and practitioners are actually asking, so the calendar answers real questions instead of guessing at themes.
  • Run three tracks, not one stream. The buyer, the admin, and the practitioner come to a workshop for different reasons; each gets its own arc instead of one generic webinar series.
  • Make follow-up part of the format. Clean capture, fast segmentation and routing, and Sales handed a specific next step per account within 48 hours, while the session is still warm.

The payoff

The workshop calendar stops being a content side-channel and becomes a pipeline instrument the Sales team plans around.

02 · Opportunity

A $60 billion spotlight is pointed at the room

SpaceX announced on June 16 that it is acquiring Anysphere, Cursor's parent, for $60 billion in stock, the largest startup acquisition on record. The deal is signed but not yet closed, expected in the third quarter, and joint model work is already being reported.

Enterprise procurement reads an acquisition as a risk question: roadmap, support continuity, governance. Those questions will walk into every live Q&A this program hosts, whether they are on the agenda or not.

A live program is where that risk becomes an asset. A press release gets read with suspicion. A host answering the hard question calmly, in public, on the record, is inspectable. I spent a decade in live newsrooms, where the answer has to hold while the camera is on.

What I'd do

  • Write the holding language once. Short, honest answers to the acquisition questions, agreed with comms and legal, so every host can deliver them live without flinching or improvising.
  • Rehearse every session like a broadcast. A run-through, speaker prep, a timing plan, and a fallback for every demo. The posting asks for exactly this discipline; it is the one a decade of live news drills into you.
  • Treat live questions as intelligence. Log what enterprise attendees actually ask, and route the pattern to Product Marketing and Sales monthly.

The payoff

When the deal closes, or wobbles, the program already has a calm, consistent voice on it, and the field stops improvising.

03 · Opportunity

This audience can smell a vendor pitch

The people these workshops serve are enterprise engineers and the leaders who came up as engineers. They convert on demonstrated craft. A generic webinar reads as marketing; a practitioner building something real reads as help.

The practitioner test

I build multi-agent systems daily, in public, and I did the enterprise version of this audience at Google: programs built from zero for 1,000+ Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers. They do not suffer marketers gladly.

career-ops is a public, cost-governed agent pipeline with a fleet of 52 scheduled agents; picture-lock is an AI film pipeline with every API call logged to a run manifest, $8.26 in the cut. The comms record holds the Google side.

The tools themselves are the proof available to this role. I would build the workshop program's own infrastructure in Cursor: the playbook repo, the registration-to-routing pipeline, the metrics dashboard. The program dogfoods the product it markets, and the build itself becomes workshop content.

What I'd do

  • Practitioner-led formats only. Live builds on real repos; slideware only where compliance demands it.
  • Coach the co-hosts. Recruit internal engineers and customer champions as speakers and prep them like on-air talent. I coached three producers from behind the camera to on-air principals; speaker development is a craft I've practiced.
  • Build the program in Cursor, visibly. The tooling doubles as content, and the audience can inspect it.

The payoff

Sessions that practitioners recommend to their managers. The room does the selling.

04 · The close

Why me

A workshop program owner is a producer, a host, an operator, and an analyst at once. The job is judgment under a live clock: picking topics that land, keeping the room honest, and proving it moved pipeline.

Here's how I plan to run the first ninety days

Day 30

By Day 30, I have inherited the machine and instrumented it.

Map every active program touching enterprise audiences: the calendar, the owners, the handoffs. Stand up the single source of truth: registration flow, attendance, follow-up status, and a pipeline baseline agreed with Sales. No new formats promised until I know what is actually running.

Day 60

By Day 60, I have shipped the first repeatable workshop wave.

One full arc owned end to end: topics from field intake, speakers prepped, a rehearsal for every session, live hosting, follow-up assets out within 48 hours, and pre/post metrics against the Day-30 baseline.

Day 90

By Day 90, workshops are provably converting champions into buyers.

Influenced pipeline visible to Sales in the numbers they already trust, and the playbook documented: templates, calendar, reporting, so the program scales without quality loss and without me as the bottleneck.

Cursor already owns the hardest asset in marketing: an audience that believes it. The workshop program is how that belief walks into the enterprise, on purpose, on a calendar.

That's the job I want, and this memo is the audition.
