How I'd approach the Fluidstack narrative.

PROVENANCE · Written July 2026, in response to a writing-sample request for Fluidstack's communications roles. Built from the public record only, sources named in place; no inside knowledge claimed or used. Where the record is unsettled, the memo says so. Reported items are labeled as reported.

A note on method: everything below is built from the public record, with sources named in place. I have no inside knowledge of Fluidstack, and I have not invented any. Where the record is unsettled, I say so, because for this company at this moment, that discipline is the narrative strategy.

Where the story stands

Fluidstack has the rarest asset in communications: a story that is true, enormous, and underexplained. Anthropic announced a roughly $50 billion American AI-infrastructure commitment in November 2025. It named Fluidstack as the partner building the custom data centers in Texas and New York, with around 800 permanent jobs, about 2,400 construction jobs, and sites coming online through 2026 (Anthropic newsroom and CNBC, November 12, 2025). A company most people had never heard of became, in one announcement, the builder behind one of the largest infrastructure commitments of the AI era.

That is the anchor. Around it sits a fast-accumulating record. A roughly $3 billion, 168-megawatt hosting agreement with Cipher Mining in Texas, with Google backstopping about $1.4 billion of the lease obligations (Cipher investor filings, September 2025). A reported $3.7 billion TeraWulf agreement at Lake Mariner in New York, Google again behind a large share (TeraWulf investor materials, August 2025). A European exascale partnership with Borealis, Dell, and NVIDIA (announced March 2025). And Bloomberg reporting in April 2026 that the company was in talks to raise about $1 billion at a roughly $18 billion valuation.

That round, as of mid-July 2026, has no primary-source confirmation of closing. So the language stays at "reported to be in talks" and nothing stronger.

The three tensions the narrative has to manage

1 · The anchor customer is both the proof and the exposure

The Anthropic build is what makes Fluidstack legible to the world. It is also a concentration story waiting for a skeptical reporter to write it. The narrative job is not to hide the concentration, it is to build the surrounding record of execution (Cipher, TeraWulf, Borealis, the poolside deployment Fluidstack itself has described) so that the Anthropic relationship reads as the flagship of a fleet rather than the fleet itself. One phrasing rule I would enforce from day one: the $50 billion is Anthropic's committed spend, never Fluidstack's revenue. The first time a company blurs that line, it spends years buying back credibility with the reporters who caught it.

2 · The neocloud sector is entering its skepticism cycle

CoreWeave, Crusoe, Lambda, and Nebius are the comparison set, and the financing structures that make gigawatt buildouts possible (lease backstops, equity stakes, project debt) are already being read two ways: as validation from the most sophisticated capital in the world, or as fragility dressed up as growth. Fluidstack cannot control which frame wins for the sector. It can control whether its own numbers are always sourced, always labeled, and never louder than the record supports. In a category where a peer's stumble becomes everyone's headline, the durable differentiator is being the company whose statements never need correcting.

3 · Speed is the brand, but permanence is the story

"Gigawatts in months" is a compelling operational claim. It is also, standing alone, indistinguishable from the hype the market has learned to discount. The stronger narrative is what the speed is for: compute as the constraint on frontier AI, and Fluidstack as the company that turns capital commitments into running clusters while competitors are still permitting. Texas and New York give this a place-based dimension that most AI companies would pay dearly for, real towns, real construction jobs, real grid interconnects. That is a story infrastructure reporters and local press will carry farther than any product announcement.

What I would actually build

Roughly one major beat per quarter since early 2025: the executive bench in February, the European exascale partnership in March, Anthropic in November, the reported round in April. That cadence is an asset. Formalize it. A narrative calendar. Every quarter lands one proof-of-execution announcement, sourced to a counterparty or a filing, so the story compounds on receipts rather than intentions.

Second, the executive voice is open ground. The leadership team's public writing footprint is thin. That's an opportunity. There is no established voice to unwind, and the sector's defining questions, power, financing structures, what frontier labs actually need from infrastructure, have few credible operator voices answering them. Build that voice, carefully, in the executives' own register. It's the sharpest editorial move on the table. A COO who helped take The Trade Desk public brings public-market discipline to how that voice should sound: specific, unsentimental, allergic to adjectives.

Third, claim discipline as infrastructure. Every number in outward copy carries a source or a label. Every superlative earns its place or dies in review. This memo practices what it proposes. The round is "reported to be in talks" because that is what Bloomberg and TechCrunch said and no primary source has said more. The TeraWulf expansion figures circulating above the initial agreement stay out until a primary confirms them. For a company whose customers are the most technically skeptical audiences in the world, and whose general counsel will read every sensitive release, the comms function should be the place where precision lives, not the place it goes to die.

Why this is the job

A first comms hire at a company like this is not a megaphone. It is a filter with taste: deciding what the record can support, saying it with a distinctive voice, and building the relationships so that when the hard story comes (and in this sector it always comes) the reporters writing it already know the company as the one that never oversold. That is the narrative I would build at Fluidstack, and the way I would build it is visible in how this memo is written.
