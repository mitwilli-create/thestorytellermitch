How I'd approach the Fluidstack narrative

Written for Fluidstack · July 2026 · Public sources only · ~8 min read

Fluidstack has built one of the most consequential companies in AI and barely said so. The gap between what it's built and what its spokespeople have said publicly is the opportunity.

What follows is a map of the openings I see and what I would do with each one in the first weeks on the job. Everything here comes from the public record. I have no inside knowledge of Fluidstack, and I haven't invented any.

01 · Opportunity

The story will get written. The only question is who frames it.

Anthropic committed $50 billion to build with Fluidstack. Overnight, a company most people couldn't name became the builder behind the biggest bet of the AI era.

But here's the exposure risk: Fluidstack's public story runs almost entirely through one customer, Anthropic. Reporters will test the concentration whether Fluidstack invites the question or not; if Anthropic slows down or builds its own way, the skeptic's version writes itself.

Now add Google. It invests in Anthropic and sells Anthropic its chips. It also backstops $1.73 billion of Fluidstack's lease obligations, an initial $1.4 billion plus another $333 million added in November 2025, when Cipher Mining, a Bitcoin miner turned AI data-center host in Texas, expanded its lease.

The money keeps circling back through the same few players in a pattern the press calls "circular financing". This reporting ultimately stokes a fear that the AI boom is propped up by insiders, not real demand. Pair this with the single customer angle, and a skeptic has the whole "house of cards" story.

CoreWeave learned this the hard way. Similar to Fluidstack, it rents AI compute to a few giant customers. In the year before its 2025 IPO, Microsoft alone made up 62 percent of its revenue.

Critics and investors (looking to make a profit on CoreWeave's stock failing publicly) claimed the company was too dependent on its one big tech customer, propped up by debt, and held together through circular deals. Their take became the version of the story everyone repeated. The stock fell about 55% from its peak while that narrative hardened.

The lesson? Tell your own story first or become the cautionary tale the whole industry points to.

What I'd do

  • Beat reporters to the punch. Bring the sourced version of the story to the 10-15 journalists who decide how the AI hardware race gets covered.
  • Keep one line sacred in every message: "the $50 billion is Anthropic's bet, not Fluidstack's bank balance."

The payoff

When the skeptical story runs, and it will, Fluidstack isn't scrambling to answer it. Its version is already the one the reporters trust.

02 · Opportunity

The whole trick is not owning the building

A new data center grid connection in Texas or New York can take three to seven years, and that wait is the single hardest problem in the entire AI buildout. A company can line up billions in funding and a warehouse of Nvidia chips in a few months, then sit for years waiting for a grid connection big enough to run them. Electricity, in one place and ready to use, is now the scarcest thing in the industry.

Fluidstack solves for this by walking into already-built Bitcoin-mining sites and re-engineering them for AI. The sites' shells are already wired for heavy power. Fluidstack brings them online as clusters built for frontier training.

The conversions are already public record. Cipher Mining in Texas and TeraWulf in upstate New York, both Bitcoin miners with power-rich sites, have signed long-term deals that hand their facilities to Fluidstack for AI. And the company increasingly pairs that reuse with selective new builds, including the custom data centers it is delivering for Anthropic.

Reuse quietly solves another problem the whole industry is about to slam into: the growing revolt against data centers. It's bigger than the industry wants to admit.

A new data center takes an enormous amount from a town and gives almost nothing back. The biggest ones employ fewer than 150 people, sometimes 25. Across the country, data centers pull more than 4 percent of the electricity and hand back about 0.01 percent of the jobs. The town carries the water and the grid, and the payoff never comes.

Fluidstack doesn't break new ground; it flips the switch on ground already broken. No new campus on farmland, no fresh load forced onto a stressed grid: it moves into buildings that already stand and power that was already flowing, old Bitcoin mines that were pulling that electricity long before AI showed up.

What I'd do

  • Get the reuse story on the record now, while no one is asking.
  • Get to know the reporters already writing the backlash story.
  • Keep a response ready. The day a "wrecking our town" piece runs, Fluidstack is already the contrast inside it.

The payoff

When the backlash reaches AI infrastructure, and it will, Fluidstack does not land on the list of targets. It becomes the standard the targets get measured against.

03 · Opportunity

Nobody is speaking…yet.

The leadership team has hardly needed to write in public yet; the work has done the talking. The voice is already there in fragments, a press-release quote here, a hiring post there. What's missing is the owned version: the signature op-ed, the podcast seat, the voice the industry quotes first.

The sector has a stack of real questions and an open seat for the credible operator who answers them: what frontier labs actually need from their infrastructure, how these enormous deals get financed, where the power really comes from.

Capital-markets scrutiny

Google, Anthropic, and the capital markets now read every number Fluidstack publishes. Only one kind of executive voice survives that audience. Specific. Plain. Allergic to hype.

A voice like that is built, not inherited. The company announced in December it would move its headquarters to New York and pointed its buildout at the US, where the reporters covering AI infrastructure read every claim against the filings. That voice doesn't exist yet; building it is the first hire's job.

The live test is already on the record.

Bloomberg reported in March that Fluidstack pulled out of a marquee 10-billion-euro data-center project in France as it pivots to larger US contracts. Credit where due: Fluidstack's rationale made it into the story, and the framing held. The miss is that it arrived reactively, inside someone else's news cycle, so the headline still led with the exit. The December headquarters announcement had already planted the US-pivot narrative; the move was to seed the France decision with two or three trusted reporters before it broke, and let consolidation, not abandonment, lead the coverage.

A comms lead who cannot narrate a reversal calmly should not be trusted with the wins either.

What I'd do

  • Bring the reporters, not just a plan. Ten years inside national newsrooms means the reporters who set the AI narrative already pick up; the trade press that covers Fluidstack weekly is the layer I'd build first.
  • Build the executive voice on a rhythm of proof. One sourced milestone a quarter, tied to a filing or a partner; Fluidstack has been landing them since early 2025, so make the habit official.
  • Put the executives into the industry debates they're sitting out, so the story compounds on evidence instead of adjectives.

The payoff

Fluidstack's leaders stop showing up only in deal announcements and become the voices the industry turns to on power, financing, and what frontier AI actually needs.

04 · The close

Why me

A first comms hire at Fluidstack doesn't just turn up the volume. The job is judgment: deciding what the record can hold, saying it in a voice people trust, and knowing the reporters and questions they will likely ask.

Here's how I plan to leverage the role in my first ninety days

Day 30

By Day 30, I have locked the sourcing rules and mapped the press.

A short, written playbook every public statement has to follow: committed spend is never called revenue, anything unconfirmed stays labeled "reported," every number ties back to a filing or a named partner. Alongside it, a map of the ten or fifteen reporters who shape how this industry gets covered, what each one cares about, and who is most likely to write the hard story first.

Day 60

By Day 60, I put Fluidstack's milestones on a calendar.

The company already lands about one real proof point a quarter: the Anthropic build-out, the Google-backstopped Cipher lease, the funding round. I'd make that a deliberate rhythm: one sourced announcement every quarter, each tied to a filing, a signed deal, or a named partner, so the story compounds on receipts instead of hype.

Day 90

By Day 90, Fluidstack has a real voice in the conversation it has been sitting out: how the compute behind frontier AI actually gets built, the power, the grid, the money.

In practice, an executive published in a tier-one outlet in their own voice, and Fluidstack is quoted as a trusted operator on the industry's biggest questions, not just in its own press releases.

Fluidstack already built the hardest thing in this industry, and gigawatts of it. The company just hasn't really told anyone yet.

That's the job I want, and this memo is the audition.
