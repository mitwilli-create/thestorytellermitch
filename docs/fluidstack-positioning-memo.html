How I'd approach the Fluidstack narrative.

Written for Fluidstack · July 2026 · Public sources only · ~8 min read

Fluidstack has built one of the most consequential companies in AI and barely said so. The gap between what it's built and what its spokespeople have said publicly is the opportunity.

What follows is a map of the openings I see and what I would do with each one in the first weeks on the job. Everything here comes from the public record. I have no inside knowledge of Fluidstack, and I haven't invented any.

01 · Opportunity

The story will get written. The only question is who frames it.

Anthropic committed $50 billion to build with Fluidstack. Overnight, a company most people couldn't name became the builder behind the biggest bet of the AI era. But here's the exposure risk: almost all of Fluidstack's business runs through one customer, Anthropic. If Anthropic slows down or builds its own way, most of the revenue goes with it.

Now add Google. It invests in Anthropic, sells Anthropic its chips, and backstops $1.73 billion of Fluidstack's leases. The money keeps circling back through the same few players in a pattern the press calls "circular financing". This reporting ultimately stokes a fear that the AI boom is propped up by insiders, not real demand. Pair this with the single customer angle, and a skeptic has the whole "house of cards" story.

CoreWeave learned this the hard way. Similar to Fluidstack, it rents AI compute to a few giant customers. When CoreWeave went public in 2025, Microsoft alone made up 62 percent of its revenue. Critics and investors (looking to make a profit on CoreWeave's stock failing publicly) claimed the company was too dependent on its one big tech customer, propped up by debt, and held together through circular deals. Their take became the version of the story everyone repeated. The narrative eventually dragged CoreWeave's stock down about 55% from its peak.

The lesson? Tell your own story first or become the cautionary tale the whole industry points to.

What I'd do

  • Beat reporters to the punch. I would bring Fluidstack's own sourced version of the story to the 10-15 journalists who crown the winners and losers of the AI hardware race, headline after headline.
  • Keep one line sacred in every message: "the $50 billion is Anthropic's bet, not Fluidstack's bank balance."

Most people applying for this job bring a plan. I bring the reporters. I spent ten years inside national newsrooms, and I already know them, and what makes them run a story.

The payoff

When the skeptical story runs, and it will, Fluidstack isn't scrambling to answer it. Its version is already the one the reporters trust.

02 · Opportunity

The whole trick is not owning the building

A new data center grid connection in Texas or New York can take three to seven years. The wait is the single hardest problem in the entire AI buildout. It's harder than raising the money and harder than getting the chips. A company can line up billions in funding and a warehouse of Nvidia chips in a few months, but then be forced to wait years for a grid connection big enough to run them. Electricity, in one place and ready to use, is now the scarcest thing in the industry.

Fluidstack solves for this scarcity, because it does not own most of what it runs. It never planned to. Fluidstack walks into already-built Bitcoin-mining sites and drops GPU clusters into shells wired for heavy power and switches them on. And, reusing instead of building quietly solves another problem the whole industry is about to slam into: the growing revolt against data centers. It's bigger than the industry wants to admit.

A new data center takes an enormous amount from a town and gives almost nothing back. The biggest ones employ fewer than 150 people, sometimes 25. Across the country, data centers pull more than 4 percent of the electricity and hand back about 0.01 percent of the jobs. The town carries the water and the grid, and the payoff never comes.

Fluidstack does not pour new campuses onto farmland or force fresh load onto a stressed grid. It moves into buildings that already stand and power that was already flowing, old Bitcoin mines that were pulling that electricity long before AI showed up. Same power, new purpose, and the rare version of this buildout a town has no reason to fight.

What I'd do

  • Get the reuse story on the record now, while no one is asking.
  • Get to know the reporters covering the data-center backlash.
  • Keep a response ready. The day a "wrecking our town" piece runs, Fluidstack is already the contrast inside it.

The payoff

When the backlash reaches AI infrastructure, and it will, Fluidstack does not land on the list of targets. It becomes the standard the targets get measured against.

03 · Opportunity

Nobody is speaking…yet.

The leadership team barely writes in public. I haven't seen any signature op-eds, podcast circuits, leadership voices to decipher. For most companies that would be a gap. For Fluidstack, that's an opening for impact.

The sector has a stack of real questions and almost no credible operator answering them: what frontier labs actually need from their infrastructure, how these enormous deals get financed, where the power really comes from.

Public-market discipline

A president who helped take The Trade Desk public knows what a trustworthy executive voice sounds like. Specific. Plain. Allergic to hype.

Rob Perdue, now Fluidstack's COO, was COO and director at The Trade Desk (2013-2019), and led it through its 2016 IPO. That is public-market discipline you can build a voice on.

What I'd do

  • Build the executive voice on a rhythm of proof. Fluidstack has landed roughly one real milestone a quarter since early 2025; I'd turn that into a habit, one sourced proof point every quarter tied to a filing or a partner.
  • Put the executives into the industry debates they're sitting out, so the story compounds on evidence instead of adjectives.

The payoff

Fluidstack's leaders stop showing up only in deal announcements and become the voices the industry turns to on power, financing, and what frontier AI actually needs.

04 · The close

Why me

A first comms hire at Fluidstack doesn't just turn up the volume. The job is judgment: deciding what the record can hold, saying it in a voice people trust, and knowing the reporters and questions they will likely ask.

Here's how I plan to leverage the role in my first ninety days:

Day 30

By Day 30, I have locked the sourcing rules and mapped the press.

A short, written playbook every public statement has to follow: committed spend is never called revenue, anything unconfirmed stays labeled "reported," every number ties back to a filing or a named partner. Alongside it, a map of the ten or fifteen reporters who shape how this industry gets covered, what each one cares about, and who is most likely to write the hard story first.

Day 60

By Day 60, I put Fluidstack's milestones on a calendar.

The company already lands about one real proof point a quarter, the exec team, the European supercomputing deal, Anthropic, the funding round. I'd make that a deliberate rhythm: one sourced announcement every quarter, each tied to a filing, a signed deal, or a named partner, so the story compounds on receipts instead of hype.

Day 90

By Day 90, Fluidstack has a real voice in the conversation it has been sitting out: how the compute behind frontier AI actually gets built, the power, the grid, the money.

In practice, an executive published in a tier-one outlet in their own voice, and Fluidstack quoted as a trusted operator on the industry's biggest questions, not just in its own press releases.

Fluidstack already built the hardest thing in this industry, and gigawatts of it. The company just hasn't really told anyone yet. That's the job I want, and this memo is the audition.
