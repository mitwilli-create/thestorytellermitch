How I'd scale NVIDIA's executive voice

Written for NVIDIA · July 2026 · Public sources only · ~7 min read

NVIDIA has the hardest executive-voice problem in tech, and it is not a shortage of things to say. The defining company of the AI era speaks constantly, to everyone at once, while its own products move faster than any comms calendar can track.

What follows is how I would think about that problem and what I would do in the first weeks on the job. Everything here comes from the public record. I have no inside knowledge of NVIDIA, and I have not invented any.

01 · The problem

One voice, every stage

In a single year, Jensen Huang opened CES in Las Vegas, headlined GTC in San Jose, took the Computex stage in Taipei, and carried GTC Paris. At Taipei he declared that "useful AI has arrived" and put Vera Rubin into full production. That is four flagship keynotes, on three continents, on top of earnings calls, op-eds, and sovereign-AI announcements.

Jensen is famous for keynoting without a script: largely unscripted, first-person, built on extended metaphors he improvises around live. The job is not writing him a speech. It is building the spine and the repeatable refrains he riffs on, and keeping them coherent across every stage. Most executive communications shops treat each speech as a one-off, a fresh brief and a blank page. A company speaking this often, holding the AI-factory and Vera-Rubin-in-production storylines steady across every appearance, cannot afford that.

What I'd do

  • Build a living voice-reference document per principal. Diction, argument logic, preferred analogies, and the framings they visibly cut in rehearsal. That observation layer is where voice absorption actually begins, not in a bio.
  • Shadow the deliverables, not the calendar. One high-stakes speech per speaker, watched end to end, tells me more than a month of meetings.

The payoff

The next keynote starts from a documented voice instead of a blank brief. The draft sounds like the leader before rehearsal, not after three rounds.

02 · The audiences

The same sentence: read by a government, an investor, and a skeptic

NVIDIA's executive voice has to land, at once, for audiences that could not be more different. A sovereign government weighing a national AI factory. An everyday investor trying to make sense of an $81.6 billion quarter and a reported trillion-dollar order book. And the engineer who can spot a hype sentence from the back of a crowded conference venue.

Calibrating the same message for each of those rooms without the voice drifting is the discipline. A number that is directional to a general audience is load-bearing to an investor. An analogy that lands in a keynote reads as spin in a filing. The voice has to bend for the audience and still be recognizably one person.

The most skeptical room in the building is the engineering audience, and it is the one I have spent the last two years writing for.

What I'd do

  • Pair every voice-reference document with a sourcing rule. Every number ties back to a filing or a named partner; anything unconfirmed stays labeled as such. Precision is what survives the skeptics.
  • Map the audiences before the messages. What a government, an investor, and an engineer each need to hear is not the same sentence louder; it is the same truth, calibrated.

The payoff

The executive voice reads as credible to the hardest audience in the room, which is the only audience whose trust the rest borrow.

03 · The stand-out criterion

Thoughtful use of AI tools, already in production

NVIDIA's job description names the thoughtful use of AI tools as a stand-out criterion. It is the part of the role most candidates will describe as an intention. I run it in production today, on exactly this problem: executive voice, at scale, for a technical audience.

Two production systems, not prototypes

A digital twin drafts VP-level content in a verified voice, held to a curated Voice DNA corpus and a banned-phrase checklist of rejected drafts. A separate triage agent handles about 60% of inbound for 1,000+ Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers.

The twin turns a day of drafting into a draft before the morning brief. The triage agent recaptures roughly 160 hours a year. Both are live: Voice OS and the comms-triage agent.

The point is not speed. The point is that a human owns the output when a senior leader's name is on it. AI scales the voice; it is never trusted to be the voice. The banned-phrase checklist exists precisely because the failure mode of these tools is fluent, confident, and slightly off, which is the one thing an executive voice cannot be.

That discipline is the same one I carried out of the newsroom: absorb a distinct editorial voice, build a production system around it, then hold the line on quality when the volume goes up. It is the exact skill the job description names.

What I'd do

  • Make the workflow legible early. An internal one-pager on AI-assisted drafting: which tools accelerate which stages, and where human judgment is non-negotiable. Shared infrastructure, not a personal black box.
  • Keep the human on the name. Every AI-assisted draft carries a verification step before it reaches a principal. The tool drafts; the writer signs.

The payoff

The team drafts at machine speed and ships at human quality. The method is legible enough that it survives any single writer leaving, including me.

04 · The close

Why me

An executive-communications hire at NVIDIA does not just turn up the volume. The job is judgment: absorbing how a leader thinks and speaks, saying it in a voice audiences trust, and building the system that keeps it consistent at the speed the company actually moves.

I spent a decade in high-stakes newsrooms absorbing how sources think before I put words in their mouths, then eight years at Google turning that instinct into documented, transferable systems: voice tooling, banned-phrase checklists, and AI-augmented drafting for principal-level executives. The gap NVIDIA has is not a shortage of talented writers. It is the infrastructure that makes good writing consistent and fast at scale.

The first ninety days

Day 30 · Absorb

A voice-reference doc for every principal on my roster.

How: shadow one high-stakes deliverable each, for diction, argument logic, and what they cut in rehearsal. Plus a one-page sourcing rule, so every number ties to a filing and nothing unconfirmed gets asserted.

Day 60 · Prove

One keynote carried brief to final, on deadline.

And: an internal one-pager on the AI-drafting workflow, so the team sees exactly where the tools help and where human judgment is non-negotiable.

Day 90 · Systematize

The whole thing becomes a repeatable onboarding kit.

The test: fewer structural edits than the last comparable speech, and the executive says it sounds like them before rehearsal, not after three rounds.

NVIDIA built the infrastructure the AI era runs on. The scarce communications skill now is not writing. It is a system that keeps the executive voice consistent at the speed the company moves.

That's the job I want, and this memo is the audition.
