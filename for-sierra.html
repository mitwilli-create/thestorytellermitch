The international record

I was on the founding team of Al Jazeera English's "The Stream," the network's social-first live show. Launch night was May 2, 2011, into 250 million households, and we tore up the rundown that evening for the bin Laden raid. The show went on to pioneer social-data journalism as an on-air format, which in 2011 was not a thing anyone had a name for.

I came back to the network years later at AJ+ as a senior producer, leading more than ten producers and building a day-of explainer production line that proved out at 50 million views. It doubled as a talent pipeline. Three of the producers I coached moved from behind the camera to on-air principals, and two of them now carry Emmy and Webby wins of their own.

In between: the daily rundown for an hour-long live program at CGTN America's Washington bureau, daily line production on Fusion's primetime flagship with Jorge Ramos through a 179% viewership-growth window, and field production from Hong Kong during the Umbrella Revolution.

Four times it was scrappy

Your job description asks for entrepreneurial, scrappy, leaning into risk, solving what gets thrown at you. Four times, with the situation, what I did, and what came of it.

  • 01 · Launching things that did not exist yet

    Situation: a social-first live news show with no format precedent, launching into 250 million households on May 2, 2011, the night the bin Laden raid broke. Action: rebuilt the rundown live, on air, and then spent the following months inventing the social-data segment as a repeatable on-air form. Result: "The Stream" became the network's proof that social could carry a live hour. Years later I did the same thing at AJ+, standing up a day-of explainer line that proved out at 50 million views.

  • 02 · Google's global remote-work shift, Q1 2020

    Situation: Google had to move its entire global workforce off-site in weeks, with no playbook, while I was writing executive communications for then-CIO Ben Fried. Action: directed the communications, content, and logistics-support strategy under continuous deadline, for a global audience of wildly mixed technical ability. Result: an 80% increase in global self-provisioning efficiency over the following quarter. People solved their own problems because the writing was good enough that they could.

  • 03 · Sourcing my own problems

    Situation: nobody at Google handed me an AI roadmap. My org was not AI-friendly, and there was real pushback. Action: found the problems myself and built on nights and weekends. A communications-triage agent with a three-prompt pipeline and touch-based routing. Then an executive-communications agent for a VP, loaded with a voice constitution and a banned-phrase verification checklist. Result: both went to production. The VP agent cut review rounds with him by about 70%. The triage agent shipped with a full operator runbook so it would outlive me. The triage agent, the voice system.

  • 04 · Building the comms function inside xGE from zero

    Situation: xGE, the org that drives technical change across Google through its 1,000+ Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers, had no communications operating model. Just ad hoc email. Action: designed a three-tier communications model and authored the multi-channel strategy, then owned the annual senior-IC summit at 600+ attendees, quarterly forums at 300+ each, and the Distinguished and Principal Engineering newsletters. Designed xGE Connects, a peer-led series where senior engineers teach the tier below. Result: a repeatable channel architecture where there had been none. Q1 2026 sessions hosted by Google DeepMind engineers drew record attendance.
