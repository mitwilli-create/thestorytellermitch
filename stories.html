Al Jazeera English · 2011

Launch night, rewritten by breaking news

The Stream was scheduled to launch on May 2, 2011. It was a new kind of live program at Al Jazeera English, built to treat Twitter, Skype, SMS, and real-time trending data as first-class journalistic sources. The night before our first broadcast, Osama bin Laden was killed in Abbottabad, Pakistan. Every piece of content we had prepared was suddenly worthless. We had roughly twelve hours to tear the launch show apart and rebuild it around a story no one had planned for, on a program that had never aired.

The infrastructure held. Our team found Sohaib Athar, a software consultant in Abbottabad who had unknowingly live-tweeted the raid from his account, @ReallyVirtual, to 751 followers. We had him on Skype by air time. As we broadcast to 250 million households, our digital producer graphed his follower count climbing in real time, 751 to 59,000, live on screen. The audience watched the internet decide that someone mattered in the same moment it happened. Nobody had a name for that kind of journalism yet.

We had made improvisation unnecessary at the system level.

The Royal Television Society named The Stream its Most Innovative Programme of 2012. What I carry from that night is more specific. We had treated the rundown as a hypothesis, permanently open to revision by incoming signal, so the worst-case scenario for a launch looked structurally like any other show day. The inputs changed. The workflow did not. That is how I still build: for the scenario you cannot anticipate, so that improvisation becomes unnecessary at the system level.

HuffPost Live · 2013

Covering Scientology, live, under legal scrutiny

In 2012 and 2013 I produced segments at HuffPost Live, a live streaming network that had been covering the Church of Scientology: booking defectors, airing testimony, running criticism the organization considered actionable. Scientology had a documented history of pursuing its critics with legal tools, and everyone in that newsroom knew it. Every segment decision, every on-screen caption, every source booking existed under the shadow of multi-million-dollar litigation scrutiny. That was not background noise. It was the operating condition for the work.

The format made it harder. HuffPost Live ran on live audience engagement, with viewer comments feeding directly into the broadcast. That loop was the platform's whole innovation, and on this coverage it was a liability. I had to build a parallel editorial filter inside it: fast enough for live pace, conservative enough to survive legal review. Defector testimony presented as personal account versus organizational fact. On-record sourcing versus implication. Every guest weighed for credibility and for exposure. All of it operational inside the broadcast window, invisible on air.

That's not caution. That's architecture.

The coverage ran. HuffPost Live did not retract, and there was no public blowback. The segments held because the risk analysis was built into the workflow before the moment of decision, not reviewed after the fact. What that period proves about how I work: I can hold two filters at once, editorial quality and institutional exposure, at live speed, without letting either collapse the other. Identify where the risk actually lives, design the filter into the system, and run it fast enough to be invisible.

HuffPost Live · 2012

The panel that aged into evidence

At HuffPost Live in 2012 I produced a panel on transgender military service. One guest was an anonymous active-duty Navy service member, no face, no surname, call sign Olivia, who joined at genuine risk of discharge for participating. At the time it felt like a Tuesday: a booking came together, a rundown slot opened, we aired it. The Pentagon would not allow transgender people to serve openly for another four years. We were naming the arguments before the policy debate had fully surfaced in mainstream coverage.

The panel around Olivia was built as a sourcing architecture, not a talking-head lineup. A trans Navy pilot. A Navy submarine veteran who founded the Transgender American Veterans Association. Counsel from the Servicemembers Legal Defense Network. A leader from OutServe. The newsroom was already operating under multi-million-dollar legal scrutiny from its Scientology coverage, so every seat needed institutional standing and on-record credibility. When a broadcast is litigation-sensitive, the panel itself is part of the editorial defense.

The story had aged into primary source documentation.

The segment ran without retraction. In June 2016 the Pentagon reversed its policy, validating what the panel had argued on air years earlier. In 2017, when the political environment reversed again, the broadcast resurfaced as archival evidence during the trans military ban debate. The story had aged into primary source documentation. That is what I build for: work that holds up under conditions nobody can predict at the time you ship it, because the sourcing and the judgment were sound from the start.

AJ+ · 2017

Fifty million views in two minutes

As a Senior Producer at AJ+ in San Francisco, I was handed a deceptively simple brief: build a video that could break through. Breaking through meant something specific. The platform was Facebook, the window was the first three seconds, and the audience would scroll past anything that felt like a lecture. The piece I led, a two-minute explainer on the US measles outbreaks, hit 50 million views. What made it work was less about creativity than about engineering.

Three levers decided everything. The hook had to answer the viewer's unasked question, why should I stop scrolling, inside the first three seconds. The argument had to be legible at zero volume, because much of Facebook autoplay ran silent, so every cut and text line carried the logic without the audio track. And the pacing had to match the platform's attention curve, not broadcast rhythm. Years at Al Jazeera English, HuffPost Live, and Fusion had trained me to think in segments and rundowns. Social video had no rundown. It had a scroll.

This was a systems problem dressed in editorial clothing.

The video earned 50 million views and more than 40,000 comments on a piece of policy journalism formatted for a two-minute window, at a network then ranked among the largest social news video publishers on Facebook. The deeper result was structural: the same-day explainer became a repeatable production line, and I coached producers from behind-camera roles into on-camera positions using it. This was a systems problem dressed in editorial clothing. Find the platform's real constraints, then build a process that works inside them every time.

#FreeAhmed Coalition · 2026

Eleven days: the #FreeAhmed coalition

In March 2026, the journalist Ahmed Shihab-Eldin, a former HuffPost Live host and one of the most recognized Arab-American broadcasters of the digital era, was detained in Kuwait on charges the international press-freedom community regarded as politically motivated. I had worked alongside Ahmed during my Al Jazeera years. By mid-April he had been held more than fifty days without resolution. The Committee to Protect Journalists took his case public, and a coalition of his former colleagues began to form around it.

I joined that coalition, drawn largely from the former HuffPost Live and AJ+ producer networks, and helped amplify the #FreeAhmed campaign across US and international channels. The effort ran alongside CPJ, the International Federation of Journalists, Amnesty International, and Ahmed's international legal team. Because a detention case carries real risk for the person at its center, the work demanded discretion: knowing what to say publicly, what to keep contained, and never becoming a vector for additional harm. I will not detail the mechanics here, deliberately.

The highest-stakes communication work is often deciding what not to say.

Ahmed was acquitted and released in late April 2026, eleven days after CPJ took the case public. I will not claim the coalition alone produced that outcome, but the pressure campaign was coordinated, international, and clean: nothing surfaced that could compromise him or the people working for his release. What it proves about how I work: a professional network is infrastructure if you maintain it, and the highest-stakes communication work is often deciding what not to say.
