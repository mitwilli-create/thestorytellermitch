AJ+ · 2017 · script as broadcast
The measles script: 125 seconds, 50 million views

Policy journalism built for a 125-second social window: a hook in the first sentence, the counter-claim named and dismantled with sourced facts, the mechanism explained in plain language, and a closing question instead of a lecture. Broadcast by AJ+ in 2017, it reached 50 million views (public source). The script runs below word for word, production cues intact.

Measles is making a comeback in the U.S. A staggering 54 cases were just confirmed in Minnesota.

Full screen: headlines from articles disproving the claim

In 1998, a British surgeon published a research paper that linked the measles, mumps, and rubella vaccine to autism. The paper was later discredited, and the doctor who published it even lost his medical license.

Source: PublicHealth.org
On camera

Since then, scientists have disproved any connection between vaccines and autism.
Full screen: anti-vaxxers protesting; Somali community in Minnesota

But anti-vaxxers have continued their campaign to convince parents that vaccinations will hurt their kids. They've even gone as far as targeting vulnerable communities. In 94% of recent Minnesota cases, the patient wasn't vaccinated.

Source: CDC
On camera

And that's a big deal for two reasons.
Full screen: measles, people infected Animated graphic: cube 1 Text on screen: "Highest risk"

Measles is one of the most contagious and lethal diseases in the world. If one person contracts the virus, it can infect more than a dozen unvaccinated people. And infants who are too young to receive the vaccination are at the highest risk.

Source: The New York Times
Full screen: vaccine footage Text on screen: "500 Americans died" · "Deafness" · "Brain damage"

Before the vaccine was developed in 1963, 500 Americans died each year because of the disease. Children who survive can still suffer lifelong complications, including deafness and brain damage.

Source: CDC
On camera Animated graphic: cube 2 Text on screen: "Herd immunity"

And when parents opt out of vaccinating their child, they affect what's called herd immunity.
Full screen: children getting vaccinated Text on screen: "Major outbreak" · "Mutate"

If enough people in a community get vaccinated, then it makes it difficult for diseases to spread. But if more than 10% of children don't get the vaccine, it may cause a major outbreak. The spreading disease could also mutate, making the vaccinations that people already have less effective.
On camera

Despite all of this, the anti-vax movement is going strong.
Close-up, punch-in to camera

So what will it take to convince these parents to vaccinate their children?
