Before and after: a hard announcement in a principal's voice

The most common failure in executive communications is a difficult decision wrapped in comfortable language. Below: a generic first draft of a platform-deprecation announcement, then the same announcement rewritten against a voice constitution for a synthetic principal, the VP of Platform Engineering at Simpler Systems, a fictional company. Both drafts were scored with voice-os in its deterministic offline mode against a three-entry synthetic corpus for that principal: overall fidelity 0.62 before, 0.75 after. The corpus and both drafts (before, after) are in the repo, so the numbers are reproducible. No Google text was used.

Before · the draft that arrives

Team, I'm excited to share an important update about our platform strategy. As part of our ongoing commitment to operational excellence, we will be sunsetting the Relay pipeline over the coming quarters. We believe this change will unlock significant efficiencies and position us for long-term success.

We understand change can be challenging, and we're committed to supporting everyone through this transition. Migration resources will be made available soon, and we encourage teams to reach out with any questions. Thank you for your continued dedication and flexibility as we work through this exciting evolution together.

After · through the voice constitution

We're shutting down Relay. New builds stop on March 1; the last supported run is June 30. If your team ships through Relay today, this is the most disruptive thing I'll ask of you this year, and I want to be straight about that instead of calling it an efficiency.

Here's the tradeoff as I weighed it: keeping Relay alive costs us the equivalent of two teams who would otherwise be working on the reliability problems you actually escalate to me. The migration guide is live now, not soon. Sam's team owns it, office hours run Tuesdays, and if your service can't make June 30, tell me by January 15 and we'll work the exception. I'd rather extend a deadline than discover a broken dependency in July.

Decision first
The "after" opens with the decision and two dates. The "before" buries the only sentence that matters under strategy language.
Name the cost
Senior engineers distrust announcements with no losers. The rewrite names who gets hurt before it names the benefit.
Dates, not adverbs
"Soon" becomes March 1, June 30, January 15, Tuesdays. Every commitment is checkable.
Hedge budget
"We believe," "excited," "exciting evolution," and "operational excellence" sit on the banned-phrase checklist. The rewrite spends its one softener where it's true: "the most disruptive thing I'll ask of you this year."
Voice markers
First person singular where the accountability lives ("tell me by January 15"), plural where the work lives. The constitution encodes that split; the gate fails drafts that get it backwards.
